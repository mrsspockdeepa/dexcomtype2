01 — The OpportunityA 25-million-person market with a retention problem hiding in plain sight

Stelo is the first OTC continuous glucose biosensor in the United States, designed for the roughly 25 million American adults living with Type 2 diabetes who don't use insulin, plus an even larger prediabetic population. It's a category-defining product — but the category itself has a known weakness: behavior-change tools driven by self-quantification tend to lose users at the one-year mark, when novelty fades, the data starts repeating itself, and the wearable becomes one more recurring expense to justify.

The clinical literature already tells us why CGM works for non-insulin Type 2: it surfaces the cause-and-effect between food, sleep, stress, movement, and glucose in a way no clinician visit ever could. But that same literature also flags the central design challenge: once the initial discovery phase is complete, the user's "job-to-be-done" changes — and most CGM apps don't change with it.

02 — Strategic ContextFour forces shaping what Stelo retention work can be — and what it can't

Before any discovery program, I'd want to be explicit about the strategic ground we're standing on. Stelo's retention problem isn't a generic SaaS retention problem. It's shaped by four forces a Principal PM has to internalize before designing the work.

Force · Competitive

The OTC CGM category is filling fast

  • Abbott Lingo (also OTC, cash-pay), Levels, January AI, NutriSense — all targeting the same non-insulin and prediabetic users.
  • "Retention" in this category isn't just about staying — it's about not switching. A churned Stelo user may be wearing a competitor's sensor next month.
  • Discovery must distinguish "lost to disengagement" from "lost to switching" — they have completely different fixes.
Force · Pharmacological

GLP-1s are reshaping the user base

  • Ozempic, Mounjaro, Zepbound adoption among non-insulin T2D is significant and growing.
  • Two scenarios for CGM value: GLP-1 obviates it (smoother curves, less interesting data), or GLP-1 creates a new co-management use case (medication efficacy, titration support).
  • Discovery must determine which scenario dominates for which segments — the answer reshapes the roadmap.
Force · Channel & Model

OTC, cash-pay is a different retention game

  • $89/month cash-pay subscription. No insurance friction reducing churn — and no insurance coverage cushioning it either.
  • HSA/FSA eligibility, employer wellness program distribution, and retail pharmacy reorder all influence retention beyond the app.
  • Acquisition channel almost certainly correlates with retention — direct.com user behaves differently than employer-program user.
Force · Regulatory

SaMD constraints shape what discovery can do

  • Stelo is FDA-cleared as a Class II Software-as-Medical-Device. This is not consumer SaaS.
  • IRB protocols govern user research touching device behavior. 510(k) implications constrain material UI changes affecting clinical interpretation.
  • Adverse event reporting (MDR), FDA cybersecurity guidance, and labeling rules all shape the experimentation framework. Methodology must operate within these — not around them.

03 — What I'd Verify FirstThe M12 cliff is my working hypothesis — here's how I'd test it on day one

I want to be honest about what I know and what I'm assuming. The "M12 cliff" framing in this case study comes from CGM behavior-change literature on adherence patterns — not from published Stelo cohort data. Stelo has only been live since mid-2024, so the M12 picture is still forming. The first week of this role isn't running new discovery. It's understanding what Dexcom already knows.

What Dexcom likely already has

Dexcom has 25+ years of CGM adherence research across G6, G7, and now Stelo. Internal UX research, clinical literature, claims data analyses, and market research likely already address many of the questions this case study raises. Doing new discovery without first inventorying that body of knowledge would be wasteful and arrogant. The right move is to figure out what's already known, what translates from prescription CGM to OTC Stelo, and what genuinely is a Stelo-specific gap that warrants new research.

The day-one data questions

Before the first new interview, here's what I'd want answered from internal data and existing research:

Q1 · Actual Curve Shape

What does Stelo's real retention curve look like by month, by cohort?

  • Where exactly does drop-off concentrate — M12, M3, or several inflection points?
  • Is it one cohort behavior or several archetypes overlaid?
  • How does it compare to G6/G7 adherence curves at equivalent tenure?
Q2 · Existing Research Inventory

What internal research already addresses these patterns?

  • Prior UXR studies on long-tenure CGM adherence — what's been learned, what translates to OTC, what doesn't.
  • Clinical affairs studies on non-insulin T2D outcomes with CGM.
  • Customer support patterns, NPS by tenure, and return reasons captured at the warehouse.
Q3 · Stelo ↔ G7 Transitions

Is some "churn" actually graduation to prescription?

  • What percentage of Stelo "churners" later show up as G7 users?
  • What percentage have a clinical event (TIR worsening, insulin start, A1c rise) coinciding with subscription end?
  • Reframing graduation as funnel success rather than churn changes the entire metric architecture.
Q4 · GLP-1 Overlap

How does the GLP-1 user behave differently?

  • What percentage of the Stelo base is on a GLP-1, and how is that growing?
  • Do GLP-1 users churn more, less, or differently?
  • Is there a GLP-1 onset → CGM disengagement signal in the data?
Q5 · Channel Mix

Does retention vary by acquisition channel?

  • Direct.com vs retail pharmacy vs employer wellness — what's the channel mix and how does it segment retention?
  • Pharmacy reorder behavior is a meaningful retention input that app analytics alone can't see.
  • Channel may turn out to be a stronger predictor than demographics.
Q6 · Literature Benchmark

How does Stelo compare to published non-insulin T2D CGM adherence?

  • ABCD CGM consensus, ADA Standards of Care updates, and recent JDST publications all bear on what "normal" adherence looks like.
  • If Stelo is outperforming the literature, the problem is different than if it's underperforming.

If the answers confirm the M12 hypothesis, the rest of this case study runs as written. If they don't — if drop-off concentrates at M3, or "churn" is mostly G7 graduation, or GLP-1 explains most of the cliff — the entire roadmap reshapes. Discovery's first job is to find out which world we're in.

04 — Problem FramingWhat we don't yet know about the people who leave

Before designing anything, I'd want to draw a clean line between the things we already know and the things we're guessing at. Discovery is most valuable when it eliminates internal pattern-matching disguised as insight.

What the data probably already shows

Sensor reorder cadence, app session frequency, in-app feature usage, support tickets, NPS by tenure. These are necessary but not sufficient — they tell us that people leave, not why.

What we almost certainly don't know yet

Whether the month-12 drop-off is one cohort or several. Whether users churn because the product failed them or because it succeeded too well — i.e., they internalized the lessons and don't feel they need it anymore. Whether the drop-off is preceded by a recoverable lapse or a hard decision. Whether financial, clinical, social, or psychological factors dominate. These are the questions discovery has to answer before the roadmap can be trusted.

"They don't leave because the product broke. They leave because the product didn't grow up with them."— A working hypothesis worth testing

05 — The Full ArcStudying the Type 2 user journey from diagnosis through drop-off, not just the in-product months

The M12 cliff doesn't exist in isolation. By the time someone reaches month 12 of Stelo, they've often been navigating Type 2 diabetes for five, ten, fifteen years — a diagnosis that has already shaped their relationship with food, their body, their healthcare system, and their identity long before they ever saw a Stelo ad. Any retention strategy that ignores that upstream context will be solving the wrong problem.

So the first thing I'd do — alongside starting the weekly interview cadence — is build a shared, living understanding of the full Type 2 user journey, from the moment of diagnosis through the moment of drop-off. The team needs the same mental model of where the user has been, not just where the user is in the product.

User Journey · From Diagnosis to Drop-Off

The arc reframes what we're studying. Instead of asking "what makes users churn at M12," we ask: which moments in this eight-phase journey were silently shaping the M12 decision all along? The user who churns at month 12 may have been set up to churn at onboarding, when the product overpromised. Or at diagnosis, when their relationship with self-quantification was first formed. Or in the coping years, when they learned to disengage from health tracking as a defense against fatigue.

Practically, this shapes the discovery work in three ways. First, our weekly interviews deliberately span every phase — including people who never bought Stelo, people who returned it within 30 days, and people newly diagnosed who haven't yet considered a CGM. Second, when we map opportunities in the Opportunity Solution Tree, we tag where in the arc they originate, which often points toward upstream interventions — onboarding repair, diagnosis-stage education partnerships, provider co-management — rather than another M12 feature. Third, the journey map itself becomes a living team artifact, updated as we learn, and the shared language for "where are we focused this quarter."

06 — Working HypothesesSeven explanations for the retention cliff — each Dexcom-specific, each pointing to a different fix

I'd enter discovery with a hypothesis tree, not a single theory. Each hypothesis points to a different intervention, so distinguishing among them isn't academic — it determines what we build first. These aren't fixed; they get pruned, replaced, and added to every week as we hear from customers. They're also deliberately Stelo-shaped — the OTC, cash-pay, non-insulin context produces churn dynamics that generic SaaS retention frameworks miss.

H1

Insight saturation

By month 12, users have learned what their body does. The app keeps showing the same patterns. The novelty curve has flattened and the product no longer earns its monthly cost.

H2

Behavior-change success paradox

Users who actually changed their habits feel they "graduated." TIR improved, their PCP took them off concern, and continuing to wear a sensor feels redundant — even though stopping is exactly when relapse risk peaks.

H3

GLP-1 obviation

User starts a GLP-1. Glucose curves flatten. The data that taught them about food now mostly confirms the medication is working. $89/month for "the medication is still working" doesn't justify itself.

H4

Graduation to prescription G7

Some "churners" aren't lost users — they've moved to prescription Dexcom G7 because their condition progressed, insulin was added, or insurance now covers it. The journey continues; our reporting shows it as loss when it's actually funnel success.

H5

Provider undermining

User brings Stelo data to their next PCP visit. The PCP — unfamiliar with OTC CGM, time-pressured, or skeptical of self-quantification — says "as long as your A1c is good, you don't need that." A single sentence from a trusted clinician ends the subscription.

H6

Wear friction compounds

Adhesive failures in hot climates, sensor errors, app sync issues, billing confusion, sleep-on-it discomfort. None alone causes churn. After the third lifted sensor in a Phoenix summer, the math shifts.

H7

Identity mismatch

The non-insulin T2D user often doesn't see themselves as "sick." Long-term, the visible badge of illness becomes harder to keep wearing — especially in social, intimate, or professional contexts where the device gets noticed.

07 — Continuous Discovery PracticeWeekly customer interviews as the heartbeat — not a one-time research phase

The biggest mistake I'd want to avoid is treating discovery as a project with a start and an end. Drawing on Teresa Torres's continuous discovery framework, I'd build the team rhythm around at least one customer interview per week, every week, indefinitely — held by a product trio of PM, designer, and engineer rotating attendance. The point of the cadence isn't volume; it's that customer evidence becomes a standing input to every decision, not a special event.

The weekly interview discipline

Each week, the trio sits with a real Stelo user — recruited continuously from a standing in-app pool with explicit consent and demographic balance targets. Interviews are story-based rather than opinion-based: we ask about specific recent experiences ("walk me through the last time you decided whether to put on a new sensor") rather than abstractions ("what features would you want?"). The difference matters because people are unreliable narrators of their preferences but reliable reporters of their behavior.

Interviews are recorded, transcribed, and synthesized into the team's Opportunity Solution Tree (Section 10). Every interview produces at least one captured opportunity — a customer need, pain, or desire — and at least one assumption that needs testing. Nothing gets built from a single interview; patterns across weeks drive prioritization.

The Dexcom org reality

The trio model in its purest Torres form ships best at growth-stage companies. At a 7,000+ person medical device firm, weekly interviews flow through Dexcom's existing UX research organization, work alongside clinical affairs and medical advisory boards, and respect the IRB protocols governing any participant interaction touching device behavior. The principle holds — direct, frequent, story-based contact between product builders and patients. The implementation gets re-engineered for the org. Part of the first 30 days in this role is mapping that internal landscape: who's already running patient research, what's the cadence, where are the gaps a product trio can fill rather than duplicate, and where are the IRB and regulatory checkpoints that govern what we can ship from learnings.

Cadence · Every Week

Trio interviews with current users

  • One 45-minute interview per week, held jointly by PM + design + engineering.
  • Recruiting prioritizes the M9–M14 risk window plus a rotating mix of long-tenure, recent churners, and reactivated users.
  • Trilingual moderators on rotation — non-English-dominant Type 2 patients are systematically under-sampled in most CGM research, and that gap costs us insight.
  • Intentional over-sampling of diabetic retinopathy, low-vision, and older users — long-tenure T2D populations have high rates of eye complications, which is precisely the cohort most at risk of disengaging from a vision-dependent app.
Cadence · Every Week

Behavioral analytics review

  • Always-on cohort dashboards: tenure curves, session frequency, reorder cadence, support sentiment.
  • Anomaly review at the start of each interview week — the data tells us who to talk to next.
  • Churn-risk signal computed daily, surfaced to the trio with feature attribution.
Cadence · Continuously

Assumption testing alongside

  • Every solution under consideration is decomposed into desirability, viability, feasibility, usability, and ethical assumptions.
  • The riskiest assumptions get small, fast tests — landing pages, prototypes, concierge experiments — before we commit engineering.
  • Assumption tests run in parallel with interviews, not sequentially after.
Cadence · Quarterly

Deeper-arc methods on demand

  • Diary studies with at-risk M8–M11 users when we need lived-experience texture interviews can't reach.
  • Provider research (PCPs, endocrinologists, CDCESs) when opportunities point at the care-team handoff.
  • Validation surveys (n ≥ 1,500) to size opportunities surfaced through interviews — qualitative finds the question, quantitative tells us how big.
Cadence · Bi-weekly

Cross-trio synthesis

  • Every two weeks, trios across the Stelo product compare what they're hearing.
  • Patterns that show up in multiple trios get elevated to the shared opportunity tree.
  • Patterns that contradict each other get an explicit decision: dig deeper, or hold and watch.
Cadence · Quarterly

Drop-off autopsy

  • Structured review of the prior quarter's churners — pulled from interviews, exit signals, and reactivation data.
  • Reviewed by product, clinical, CX, and growth leadership together.
  • Output: which opportunities have shifted in priority, which assumptions held, which got refuted.

"The point of weekly interviews isn't to gather data faster. It's to make customer evidence ambient — so it's already in the room when product decisions get made."— Adapted from Teresa Torres, Continuous Discovery Habits

08 — The Retention Moments ModelReframing the journey as a sequence of jobs, not a single subscription

Retention isn't a property of the product — it's the cumulative result of how well the product matches the user's job at each stage. The "job" of a CGM at month 1 is not the same job at month 12.

User Journey · Job-to-be-Done by Tenure

The Discover and Learn stages are where Stelo's current experience excels — pattern recognition, novelty, and clear "aha" moments. The Maintain and Decide stages are where the product has to do more work, and where the discovery findings will either confirm or challenge what's currently shipping.

09 — Segmentation HypothesisThe "T2D non-insulin" label hides at least four distinct retention archetypes

One of the most common failure modes in healthcare products is treating the diagnostic label as the segment. "Type 2, non-insulin" is a regulatory category, not a behavioral one. Discovery should validate or refute the segmentation below, then prioritize roadmap by segment value × addressability.

Working Segment Primary Job Drop-off Signal Likely Lever
The Newly Diagnosed Make sense of the diagnosis; build a coping system. Information overload, anxiety spikes around M3–M6. Education pacing, emotional support, clinician handoff.
The Disciplined Optimizer Hit a target A1c through controlled experimentation. Hits target by M9; questions ongoing value. Long-arc challenges, advanced metrics, identity reinforcement.
The GLP-1 Co-User Pair CGM with weight-loss medication for visible feedback. Stops sensor when medication produces weight loss. Co-managed program; reposition CGM as durability insurance.
The Reluctant Wearer Comply with provider/family pressure; minimize visibility. Adhesive issues, social discomfort, low engagement throughout. Form factor, discretion features, reframe around pleasure not duty.

10 — Opportunity Solution TreeHow weekly interviews actually drive the roadmap

Continuous discovery without a structure for synthesis becomes a stream of anecdotes. The Opportunity Solution Tree is how the trio translates what we hear in interviews into prioritized roadmap bets — with an explicit chain from outcome to opportunity to solution to assumption test. The tree is a living document, updated weekly, and is the team's single source of truth for what we know, what we're betting on, and what we're testing next.

Opportunity Solution Tree · Living Artifact, Updated Weekly

The tree shown is a simplified view. In practice, every opportunity branches into multiple solution candidates, and every solution decomposes into the riskiest desirability, viability, feasibility, usability, and ethical assumptions that have to hold. The tree's job isn't to be comprehensive — it's to make the team's reasoning visible and contestable. When a stakeholder asks "why are we building this and not that," the answer points to the tree, the interviews behind it, and the tests we've already run.

How prioritization actually happens

Opportunities are weighed by reach (how many users in which segments), severity (how acute the pain), and frequency (how often it surfaces). Solutions are prioritized by retention impact estimate, assumption risk, and engineering cost. The trio commits to a small number of bets per quarter, runs assumption tests in weeks rather than months, and updates the tree based on what we learn. Anything that survives the tree gets built; anything that doesn't gets parked with a clear note on what would have to change.

11 — Discovery Outputs & Likely RequirementsWhat the program delivers — and what platform requirements would likely emerge

Committing to specific platform requirements before discovery is the same trap as committing to features before research. So this section captures two things: (a) the artifacts the discovery program will produce by day 90 regardless of what it finds, and (b) the platform requirements likely to emerge given the working hypotheses, with explicit conditional framing on each.

Day-90 deliverables

Independent of what discovery finds, the program produces: a validated retention hypothesis tree with evidence weighting per hypothesis; a living Opportunity Solution Tree updated weekly; either validated working segmentation or its refutation; a prioritized portfolio of retention bets with assumption tests in flight; and the standing continuous discovery infrastructure (recruiting, cadence, synthesis tooling, drop-off autopsy ritual) for the team to keep using.

Likely platform requirements (each conditional)

The requirements below are the shape of what would likely emerge — not commitments. Performance bars, scope, and priority all get set after discovery sees the data, not before.

FR-01 · Churn Risk Signal · Conditional on H1, H6 patterns

A propensity-to-lapse signal computed continuously per user

  • Likely inputs: session frequency trend, sensor reorder cadence, support contact sentiment, in-app feature drop-off, days since last "aha" event, tenure, claimed segment.
  • Likely output: per-user lapse risk score, refreshed daily, with feature attribution for the top three drivers.
  • Performance bar: set post-discovery once we know signal density, class balance, and what intervention class is being gated. Generic precision/recall targets pre-committed before seeing data are theater, not engineering.
  • Eval & guardrails: calibration audited monthly across age, gender, ethnicity, and language to surface disparate accuracy. No automated subscription action without human-in-the-loop. Any model touching device behavior cleared through clinical affairs and SaMD review.
FR-02 · Segment Assignment Service · Conditional on segmentation validating

A behaviorally derived segment classifier exposed to all surfaces

  • Source signals: onboarding intake, first-30-day behavioral fingerprint, claimed comorbidities, medication context (where consented).
  • Output contract: a primary segment label plus confidence; segments are mutable and re-evaluated quarterly.
  • Consumers: in-app content engine, lifecycle messaging, support tooling, clinician portal.
  • Auditability: every segment change is logged with the triggering signal so clinical and CX teams can investigate.
FR-03 · Adaptive Insight Engine · Likely needed regardless

The app's insight content has to evolve with tenure, not repeat

  • Content library tagged by tenure stage (Discover / Learn / Maintain / Decide) and segment.
  • Insight cadence throttles automatically — a long-tenure user who has seen the "stress raises glucose" lesson five times should not see it a sixth.
  • New insight types added at predictable intervals: trend comparison, peer-cohort benchmarks (privacy-preserved), seasonal pattern recall, year-over-year reflection.
  • Editorial guardrails: no insight is generated for a user without sufficient supporting data; uncertainty surfaced explicitly.
FR-04 · Care-Team Hand-off Surface · Priority depends on H5 (Provider Undermining)

A lightweight provider-facing artifact users can actually share

  • One-tap export of a clinically meaningful summary (TIR, GMI, time-above-range, behavioral notes) in a format providers will actually open.
  • Configurable sharing — ongoing read-only link, point-in-time PDF, or fax-friendly export for older practices.
  • Bidirectional: a provider note or check-in goal flows back into the app and influences the insight engine.
  • HIPAA-aligned consent flow with clear scope, revocation, and audit log.
FR-05 · Re-Engagement Experimentation Framework · Adapted for SaMD constraints

Infrastructure to test retention bets without compromising clinical or regulatory integrity

  • Cohort-based assignment with clean holdouts across the M9–M14 risk window.
  • Pre-registered hypotheses, primary metric, and stop conditions before any test ships.
  • Asymmetric stop rules: experiments that move retention without affecting clinical outcomes proceed; those with even small adverse signal pause for review.
  • SaMD discipline: tests touching glucose interpretation, alerts, or anything affecting clinical decisions go through clinical affairs review and 510(k) impact assessment before launch — not consumer A/B testing.
  • Test ledger visible to clinical, legal, regulatory, and design — every running test, what it's measuring, who owns it.
FR-06 · Continuous Discovery Infrastructure

Weekly customer interviews as a non-negotiable team practice, with the tooling to make them frictionless

  • Recruiting pipeline: standing in-app pool that delivers ≥1 qualified, consented user per week per trio, with demographic balance targets and trilingual coverage.
  • Story-based interview kit: shared moderator training, question library, recording & transcription pipeline; first 30 days of new-PM onboarding includes joining four interviews.
  • Living Opportunity Solution Tree: the team's source of truth, updated weekly by the trio, visible to leadership and stakeholders without translation.
  • Assumption test queue: every active solution has its riskiest assumptions logged, prioritized by risk × evidence, with a designated test owner.
  • Cross-trio synthesis ritual: bi-weekly pattern review across product surfaces; quarterly drop-off autopsy reviewed by product, clinical, and CX leadership.

12 — Success MetricsWhat good looks like — and what to be skeptical of

Retention metrics are easy to game. If the only thing we measure is M12+ retention rate, we'll find ways to inflate it that don't serve users. The metric architecture has to balance growth, clinical, and experiential outcomes.

North Star
Retained, Engaged Users at M18
Users still on subscription and showing evidence of ongoing behavioral engagement (not dormant subscribers). Tenure past the cliff is the real test.
Clinical Companion
Sustained TIR / GMI Improvement
TIR (time-in-range) and GMI (glucose management indicator) are captured directly in Stelo and trackable per cohort over time. A1c — the gold-standard validation — requires clinical data linkage that doesn't exist in Stelo today; building that pathway is itself a discovery output.
Leading Indicator
M9 Engagement Health Score
Composite of session frequency trend, feature breadth, insight engagement, and reorder timing — used to validate interventions early.
Equity Guardrail
Retention Parity Across Segments
Retention gains have to materialize across age, language, geography, and income segments — not just the easiest-to-reach ones.
Experience
Voluntary Reactivation Rate
Of users who do churn, what percentage return within six months on their own initiative? A strong product earns its way back.
Trust
"Helpful Now" NPS by Tenure
Tracked monthly per cohort. Watch for the M9–M12 dip before it shows up in churn data.

13 — Risks & GuardrailsWhat I'd watch for, and how I'd protect against it

Risk · Clinical

Optimizing for retention at the expense of clinical outcomes

If retention bets nudge users toward continued use without clinical benefit, we've built a habit, not a health product. Every retention experiment runs paired with a clinical-outcome check, and clinical metrics have veto power on launch.

Risk · Equity

Personalization that widens disparities

Segment-aware features can deliver less to under-represented users if training data is skewed. Discovery includes intentional over-recruiting from under-served populations; segmentation models are audited for performance parity before any user-facing rollout.

Risk · Privacy

Health data feeding the wrong loops

Glucose data is sensitive even when de-identified. Care-team sharing, peer benchmarks, and ML training each require their own explicit consent surface, with revocation that actually deletes downstream artifacts.

Risk · Discovery

Confirmation bias in the synthesis

Seven hypotheses, run by people with priors, will tend to find what they expect. Synthesis uses a structured framework where each hypothesis must be weighted with both confirming and disconfirming evidence before any roadmap call is made.

Risk · Competitive

Optimizing retention against the wrong opponent

Users don't just stay or leave — they switch. Retention work that doesn't account for Abbott Lingo, Levels, or other OTC alternatives may move the wrong metric. Discovery includes "did you try or consider anything else" as a standard probe, and the metric architecture distinguishes "lost to switching" from "lost entirely."

Risk · Pharmacological Disruption

GLP-1 reshaping the use case under us

If GLP-1 adoption accelerates and CGM value drops correspondingly for that segment, retention work focused on insight features may be working against a structural headwind. The roadmap has to include an explicit GLP-1 strategy — co-management, titration support, post-medication-titration monitoring — not treat it as a side note.

Risk · Internal Cannibalization

Stelo retention at the expense of G7 graduation

If we build features that keep clinically progressing users on Stelo who would otherwise transition to prescription Dexcom G7, we may optimize against the larger-revenue prescription business. Cross-portfolio metric reviews catch this; roadmap calls explicitly weigh Stelo retention vs. G7 funnel health, and "graduation to G7" is treated as a positive outcome, not a churn event.
