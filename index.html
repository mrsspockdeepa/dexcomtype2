01. The RoleProblem discovery for the full Type 2 Patient Experience portfolio

The role spans Dexcom's complete Type 2 patient experience: Stelo's OTC, cash-pay non-insulin segment; G7 Type 2 prescription users (insulin and non-insulin); and future Type 2 products. The mandate is to identify the highest-impact customer and business problems before solution investment is made, frame them in terms of impact rather than features, and produce decision-ready evidence that informs vertical product, platform, data, and UX teams.

This is a problem-discovery role, not a build role. Solution discovery and delivery remain with the vertical teams. My job is to clarify the why, define success, and supply the evidence those teams need to make good roadmap calls.

This case study uses one flagship problem, the long-term retention cliff in non-insulin Type 2 CGM use, as a worked example of how I'd run discovery in the first six months. The methodology generalizes; the M12 hypothesis is the test case.

02. Strategic ContextFour forces shaping what Stelo retention work can be, and what it can't

Before any discovery program, I'd want to be explicit about the strategic ground we're standing on. Stelo's retention problem isn't a generic SaaS retention problem. It's shaped by four forces a Principal PM has to internalize before designing the work.

Force · Competitive

The OTC CGM category is filling fast

  • Abbott Lingo (also OTC, cash-pay), Levels, January AI, NutriSense. All targeting the same non-insulin and prediabetic users.
  • "Retention" in this category isn't just about staying. It's about not switching. A churned Stelo user may be wearing a competitor's sensor next month.
  • Discovery must distinguish "lost to disengagement" from "lost to switching". They have completely different fixes.
Force · Pharmacological

GLP-1s are reshaping the user base

  • Ozempic, Mounjaro, Zepbound adoption among non-insulin T2D is significant and growing.
  • Two scenarios for CGM value: GLP-1 obviates it (smoother curves, less interesting data), or GLP-1 creates a new co-management use case (medication efficacy, titration support).
  • Discovery must determine which scenario dominates for which segments, the answer reshapes the roadmap.
Force · Channel & Model

OTC, cash-pay is a different retention game

  • $89/month cash-pay subscription. No insurance friction reducing churn, and no insurance coverage cushioning it either.
  • HSA/FSA eligibility, employer wellness program distribution, and retail pharmacy reorder all influence retention beyond the app.
  • Acquisition channel almost certainly correlates with retention. Direct.com user behaves differently than employer-program user.
Force · Regulatory

SaMD constraints shape what discovery can do

  • Stelo is FDA-cleared as a Class II Software-as-Medical-Device. This is not consumer SaaS.
  • IRB protocols govern user research touching device behavior. 510(k) implications constrain material UI changes affecting clinical interpretation.
  • Adverse event reporting (MDR), FDA cybersecurity guidance, and labeling rules all shape the experimentation framework. Methodology must operate within these, not around them.

03. What I'd Verify FirstThe M12 cliff is my working hypothesis. Here's how I'd test it on day one

I want to be honest about what I know and what I'm assuming. The "M12 cliff" framing in this case study comes from CGM behavior-change literature on adherence patterns, not from published Stelo cohort data. Stelo has only been live since mid-2024, so the M12 picture is still forming. The first week of this role isn't running new discovery. It's understanding what Dexcom already knows.

What Dexcom likely already has

Dexcom has 25+ years of CGM adherence research across G6, G7, and now Stelo. Internal UX research, clinical literature, claims data analyses, and market research likely already address many of the questions this case study raises. Doing new discovery without first inventorying that body of knowledge would be wasteful and arrogant. The right move is to figure out what's already known, what translates from prescription CGM to OTC Stelo, and what genuinely is a Stelo-specific gap that warrants new research.

The day-one data questions

Before the first new interview, here's what I'd want answered from internal data and existing research:

Q1 · Actual Curve Shape

What does Stelo's real retention curve look like by month, by cohort?

  • Where exactly does drop-off concentrate. M12, M3, or several inflection points?
  • Is it one cohort behavior or several archetypes overlaid?
  • How does it compare to G6/G7 adherence curves at equivalent tenure?
Q2 · Existing Research Inventory

What internal research already addresses these patterns?

  • Prior UXR studies on long-tenure CGM adherence. What's been learned, what translates to OTC, what doesn't.
  • Clinical affairs studies on non-insulin T2D outcomes with CGM.
  • Customer support patterns, NPS by tenure, and return reasons captured at the warehouse.
Q3 · Stelo ↔ G7 Transitions

Is some "churn" actually graduation to prescription?

  • What percentage of Stelo "churners" later show up as G7 users?
  • What percentage have a clinical event (TIR worsening, insulin start, A1c rise) coinciding with subscription end?
  • Reframing graduation as funnel success rather than churn changes the entire metric architecture.
Q4 · GLP-1 Overlap

How does the GLP-1 user behave differently?

  • What percentage of the Stelo base is on a GLP-1, and how is that growing?
  • Do GLP-1 users churn more, less, or differently?
  • Is there a GLP-1 onset → CGM disengagement signal in the data?
Q5 · Channel Mix

Does retention vary by acquisition channel?

  • Direct.com vs retail pharmacy vs employer wellness. What's the channel mix and how does it segment retention?
  • Pharmacy reorder behavior is a meaningful retention input that app analytics alone can't see.
  • Channel may turn out to be a stronger predictor than demographics.
Q6 · Literature Benchmark

How does Stelo compare to published non-insulin T2D CGM adherence?

  • ABCD CGM consensus, ADA Standards of Care updates, and recent JDST publications all bear on what "normal" adherence looks like.
  • If Stelo is outperforming the literature, the problem is different than if it's underperforming.

If the answers confirm the M12 hypothesis, the rest of this case study runs as written. If they don't, if drop-off concentrates at M3, or "churn" is mostly G7 graduation, or GLP-1 explains most of the cliff, the entire approach reshapes. Discovery's first job is to find out which world we're in.

04. The Full ArcStudying the Type 2 patient journey end-to-end, from diagnosis through drop-off

The M12 cliff doesn't exist in isolation. By the time someone reaches month 12 of Stelo, they've often been navigating Type 2 diabetes for five, ten, fifteen years, a diagnosis that has already shaped their relationship with food, their body, their healthcare system, and their identity long before they ever saw a Stelo ad. Any retention work that ignores that upstream context solves the wrong problem.

Alongside starting the weekly interview cadence, the early work is building a shared, living understanding of the full Type 2 patient journey, from diagnosis through drop-off. The team needs the same mental model of where the user has been, not just where the user is in the product.

User Journey · From Diagnosis to Drop-Off

The arc reframes what we're studying. Instead of asking "what makes users churn at M12," we ask: which moments in this eight-phase journey silently shaped the M12 decision all along? The user who churns at month 12 may have been set up to churn at onboarding, at diagnosis, or in the coping years long before Stelo entered the picture.

Practically, this shapes the discovery work in three ways. Weekly interviews span every phase, including people who never bought Stelo and people newly diagnosed who haven't yet considered a CGM. Problems get tagged with where in the arc they originate, which often points toward upstream interventions: onboarding repair, diagnosis-stage education partnerships, provider co-management, rather than another M12 feature. The journey map itself becomes a living team artifact and the shared language for where we're focused.

05. Working Problem HypothesesSeven candidate explanations for the cliff, each pointing to a different problem to validate

I'd enter discovery with a hypothesis tree, not a single theory. Each hypothesis frames a different business problem and demands a different evidence package to confirm or rule out. These aren't fixed; they get pruned, replaced, and added to every week as we hear from customers. They're also deliberately Stelo-shaped: the OTC, cash-pay, non-insulin context produces churn dynamics that generic SaaS retention frameworks miss.

H1

Insight saturation

By month 12, users have learned what their body does. The app keeps showing the same patterns. The novelty curve has flattened and the product no longer earns its monthly cost.

H2

Behavior-change success paradox

Users who actually changed their habits feel they "graduated." TIR improved, their PCP took them off concern, and continuing to wear a sensor feels redundant, even though stopping is exactly when relapse risk peaks.

H3

GLP-1 obviation

User starts a GLP-1. Glucose curves flatten. The data that taught them about food now mostly confirms the medication is working. $89/month for "the medication is still working" doesn't justify itself.

H4

Graduation to prescription G7

Some "churners" aren't lost users. They've moved to prescription Dexcom G7 because their condition progressed, insulin was added, or insurance now covers it. The journey continues; our reporting shows it as loss when it's actually funnel success.

H5

Provider undermining

User brings Stelo data to their next PCP visit. The PCP, unfamiliar with OTC CGM, time-pressured, or skeptical of self-quantification, says "as long as your A1c is good, you don't need that." A single sentence from a trusted clinician ends the subscription.

H6

Wear friction compounds

Adhesive failures in hot climates, sensor errors, app sync issues, billing confusion, sleep-on-it discomfort. None alone causes churn. After the third lifted sensor in a Phoenix summer, the math shifts.

H7

Identity mismatch

The non-insulin T2D user often doesn't see themselves as "sick." Long-term, the visible badge of illness becomes harder to keep wearing, especially in social, intimate, or professional contexts where the device gets noticed.

06. The 6-Month Discovery PlanMonth-by-month progression from internal inventory to decision-ready problem maps

The biggest mistake to avoid is treating discovery as a project with a start and an end. Drawing on Teresa Torres's continuous discovery framework, the rhythm is built around at least one customer interview per week, every week, indefinitely, held by a product trio of PM, designer, and engineer. The first six months layer a progression on top of that rhythm: inventory what's known, validate the hypothesis tree, surface and pressure-test the highest-impact problems, then hand decision-ready evidence to the vertical product, platform, data, and UX teams that will act on it.

First Six Months · Discovery Plan

The Dexcom org reality

The Torres trio model in its purest form ships best at growth-stage companies. At a 7,000+ person medical device firm, weekly interviews flow through Dexcom's existing UX research organization, work alongside clinical affairs and medical advisory boards, and respect the IRB protocols governing any participant interaction touching device behavior. The first 30 days are partly about mapping that landscape: who's already running patient research, what's the cadence, where are the gaps a product trio can fill rather than duplicate, and where are the regulatory checkpoints.

The weekly rhythm in practice

Cadence · Every Week

Trio interviews

  • One 45-minute story-based interview, held jointly by PM + design + engineering.
  • Recruiting prioritizes the M9–M14 risk window plus rotating long-tenure, churners, reactivated users.
  • Trilingual moderators on rotation; intentional over-sampling of diabetic retinopathy and low-vision users.
Cadence · Every Week

Behavioral analytics review

  • Always-on cohort dashboards: tenure curves, session frequency, reorder cadence, support sentiment.
  • Anomaly review tells the trio who to talk to next.
  • Churn-risk signal computed daily, surfaced with feature attribution.
Cadence · Continuously

Assumption testing in parallel

  • Each problem hypothesis decomposed into desirability, viability, feasibility, usability, ethical assumptions.
  • Riskiest assumptions get small, fast tests: landing pages, prototypes, concierge experiments.
  • Tests run alongside interviews, not sequentially after.
Cadence · Quarterly

Drop-off autopsy

  • Structured review of the prior quarter's churners, pulled from interviews, exit signals, reactivation data.
  • Reviewed by product, clinical, CX, and growth leadership together.
  • Output: which problems shifted in priority, which assumptions held, which got refuted.

"The point of weekly interviews isn't to gather data faster. It's to make customer evidence ambient, so it's already in the room when product decisions get made."Adapted from Teresa Torres, Continuous Discovery Habits

07. Problem Tree & Assumption TestsHow weekly interviews translate into decision-ready evidence

Continuous discovery without a structure for synthesis becomes a stream of anecdotes. The Problem Tree is how the trio translates what we hear in interviews into prioritized, decision-ready evidence, with an explicit chain from outcome to customer problem to the assumptions we'd need to test to know whether the problem is real, prevalent, and high-impact. The tree is a living document, updated weekly, and is the team's single source of truth for what we know, what we're betting on, and what we're testing next. Solutions live downstream of this tree, owned by vertical product and platform teams.

Problem Tree · Living Artifact, Updated Weekly

The tree shown is a simplified view. In practice, every problem decomposes into the riskiest assumptions that have to hold for it to be real and high-impact. The tree's job is to make the team's reasoning visible and contestable. When a stakeholder asks "why are we focused on this and not that," the answer points to the tree, the interviews behind it, and the tests that surfaced or refuted each problem.

How prioritization actually happens

Problems are weighed by reach (how many users in which segments), severity (how acute the pain), frequency (how often it surfaces), and business impact (LTV at risk, retention revenue). Assumption tests are prioritized by the riskiness of the assumption: what would we need to believe for this problem to matter, and how cheaply can we falsify it? Solutions, prioritization of solutions, and roadmap calls all live downstream of this tree, with the vertical product teams that own those surfaces.

08. Discovery Deliverables & InfluenceWhat the work produces, and how it flows into other teams' decisions

This role doesn't ship platform features. It produces decision-ready evidence that informs the vertical product, platform, data, UX, and operations teams that build. Below: what's produced, and where each artifact lands.

What the discovery program ships

D1 · Validated Problem Hypothesis Tree

The seven hypotheses, each tagged with evidence weighting

  • Each hypothesis labeled confirmed, refuted, or under investigation, with the qualitative themes and quantitative signals supporting that call.
  • Updated weekly. Stakeholders can see what we believe today and why.
D2 · Living Problem Tree

Outcome → problems → assumption tests, always current

  • The Section 07 artifact, kept live. Source of truth for what we know, what we're betting on, what's being tested next.
  • Visible to leadership and stakeholders without translation.
D3 · Decision-Ready Problem Maps

One per high-impact problem, framed as impact, not features

  • For each validated problem: scope, affected segments, business impact (LTV at risk, retention revenue), evidence base, success guardrails, open assumption tests.
  • The package vertical product teams need to make their own roadmap call with confidence.
D4 · Continuous Voice-of-Customer Pipeline

Standing infrastructure, not a one-time program

  • Recruiting pool, weekly interview cadence, behavioral cohort dashboards, drop-off autopsies.
  • Surfaces emerging problems before they show up in churn data.

Cross-functional influence model

Each deliverable has a clear receiving team and a clear way of influencing their decisions. The role's leverage comes from making problem evidence ambient across the org, not from controlling any team's roadmap.

10. Risks & GuardrailsWhat I'd watch for, and how I'd protect against it

Risk · Discovery Method

Confirmation bias in synthesis

Seven hypotheses, run by people with priors, will tend to find what they expect. Synthesis uses a structured framework where each hypothesis must be weighted with both confirming and disconfirming evidence before any decision-ready problem map ships.

Risk · Pharmacological Disruption

GLP-1 reshaping the use case under us

If GLP-1 adoption accelerates and CGM value drops for that segment, retention work focused on insight features may be working against a structural headwind. Discovery treats GLP-1 explicitly as both a hypothesis and a strategic context, not a side note.

Risk · Internal Cannibalization

Stelo retention at the expense of G7 graduation

If problem maps favor keeping clinically progressing users on Stelo who would otherwise transition to prescription Dexcom G7, we may optimize against the larger prescription business. Cross-portfolio reviews surface this; "graduation to G7" is treated as a positive outcome, not churn.

Risk · Clinical

Optimizing retention at the expense of clinical outcomes

If retention work nudges users toward continued use without clinical benefit, we've informed the team toward a habit, not a health product. Every problem map is paired with clinical outcome implications, and clinical metrics have veto power on what gets prioritized.
